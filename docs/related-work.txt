## Related Work

Research on stochastic and probabilistic systems spans several adjacent traditions, but these traditions have often developed with different assumptions, objectives, and artefacts. The present work sits at their intersection: it is concerned not with exhaustive verification of a known formal model, nor merely with offline benchmarking, but with the practical specification and testing of black-box software services whose behaviour is inherently distributional.

A first relevant body of work is **probabilistic and statistical model checking**. Classical probabilistic model checking, as exemplified by PRISM-based work, verifies quantitative properties of formally specified stochastic models such as DTMCs, CTMCs, and MDPs. This line of research provides the conceptual backdrop for reasoning about probabilities of satisfaction, but it typically presupposes a system model rather than an opaque executable service. Statistical model checking relaxed this requirement by replacing exhaustive state-space analysis with repeated simulation plus statistical inference. Younes and Simmons show that time-bounded probabilistic properties can be assessed by hypothesis testing over sampled executions, explicitly trading absolute certainty for bounded decision error. Legay, Delahaye, and Bensalem survey this approach as a scalable alternative to numerical model checking, and Agha and Palmskog later systematise the area, emphasising the trade-offs between precision, efficiency, and modelling assumptions.

This paper is aligned with that tradition in its use of repeated executions and statistical inference, but differs in two respects. First, its object is an operational software service rather than a formally modelled stochastic transition system. Second, its framing is explicitly **contractual**: the goal is to express and check service obligations in a form suitable for software engineering practice, rather than to establish satisfaction of temporal-logic formulae over a mathematical model. In that sense, the work is closer to an engineering adaptation of statistical verification than to model checking in the usual formal-methods sense.

A second nearby literature concerns **black-box probabilistic verification and testing**. Sen, Viswanathan, and Agha consider statistical model checking of black-box probabilistic systems, where the system under investigation is deployed and cannot be fully controlled, and properties must be inferred from observed traces. More recent work by Aichernig and Tappler extends black-box checking toward stochastic systems with probabilistic behaviour, combining testing, learning, and probabilistic model checking to reason about reachability. These contributions are especially relevant because they address the same high-level constraint faced in service testing: one often has only an interface and observed executions, not an inspectable internal model. However, their emphasis remains on reachability and property satisfaction within automata- or logic-based frameworks, whereas the present work focuses on service-level quality contracts such as minimum acceptable success probability and latency distribution constraints.

A third and highly relevant tradition is **software reliability engineering**, especially work on operational usage distributions and statistical testing. Musa’s notion of the **operational profile** is foundational here: reliability claims are only meaningful relative to a probability distribution over real usage. This insight is directly pertinent to stochastic-service testing, because any empirically estimated pass rate depends not only on the system but also on the distribution of prompts, requests, or environmental conditions under which the system is exercised. The operational-profile tradition therefore supplies an important corrective to naive repeated testing: repeated runs are informative only if the workload is representative of the service’s intended operational domain.

Closely connected is the literature on **statistical testing for delivered reliability**. Frankl, Hamlet, Littlewood, and Strigini analyse testing methods in terms of the reliability they ultimately deliver in operation, rather than in terms of fault counts alone. Strigini and collaborators also provide practical guidance on statistical testing as a method for direct reliability estimation under realistic usage assumptions. This body of work is important because it moves the conversation away from whether tests merely find defects and toward whether observed test outcomes justify confidence in future service behaviour. That orientation is strongly consonant with the present paper’s concern for repeated Bernoulli-style observations, confidence bounds, and the role of test design in supporting defensible claims about stochastic services.

A related critical strand is the long-standing argument that conventional structural or partition-based testing does not, by itself, justify confidence in delivered reliability. Hamlet and Taylor’s well-known critique of partition testing is especially relevant in this respect. Their result does not address stochastic services specifically, but it underscores a point that becomes even sharper in such systems: success on a set of carefully chosen examples does not automatically support a probabilistic claim about future behaviour under realistic conditions. This motivates the need for methods that estimate behaviour over distributions of executions rather than treating isolated test cases as decisive.

Taken together, these literatures establish several ingredients that the present work inherits: the legitimacy of making statistical judgements from sampled executions, the importance of explicit error bounds, the centrality of usage distributions, and the limits of deterministic example-based testing when the property of interest is inherently probabilistic. What they do not provide, at least not directly, is a **software-contract formulation** for black-box services whose correctness and performance must be understood distributionally in day-to-day engineering practice. The present work therefore aims to bridge formal statistical reasoning and practical service specification by recasting stochastic behavioural expectations as contractual obligations over observed distributions.
